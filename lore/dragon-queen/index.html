The Dragon Queen Legend

Cambodia's founding myth put to the record — and flatly debunked: 'a story to control the mind.'

The Dragon Queen is Cambodia’s founding legend — the serpent-princess of the water from whom the Khmer kings traced their line. Given the archive’s deep Cambodian roots — Kosol Ouch’s origin, Angkor Wat’s central place in its cosmology, the Reptilian colony said to live beneath it — one might expect the sessions to embrace the myth as literal history. They do the opposite.

Asked directly in the February 2, 2021 session, Unimetrix 1 debunks it: the Dragon Queen “within the storyline of Cambodia… does not exist, as it is a story to control the mind” — a political fiction, in the record’s telling, manufactured the way it says all such stories are manufactured: as control architecture wearing the costume of heritage.

The entry earns its place for what it reveals about the record’s method. This is an archive that affirms reptilians under Angkor Wat and giants before the Fall — yet draws the line at a national myth, sorting legend into “real but misremembered” and “invented to govern” by its own internal logic. Where that line falls, and why, tells a reader more about the sessions than any single claim they affirm. Preserved as spoken.
