Teleportation

Jump rooms, jumpgates, and fifteen minutes to Mars — instantaneous transit as the archive's established fact.

Codex entryTechnologies & Devices

Teleportation is treated throughout the archive as an established, working capability — and it comes with named infrastructure and named travelers. Andy Basiago’s testimony supplies the human record: Project Pegasus teleporting children across the country from 1968, and the 1980s “jump room” in El Segundo that put him on Mars — “on Mars in 15 minutes… red dirt on his shoes” — roughly forty times. The wider network the sessions describe is a system of jumpgates: Mars, Moon, and Jupiter gates, guarded assets of the Secret Space Program, serious enough (the interviews claim) for people to die protecting them.

Unimetrix 1 extends the capability to the personal scale: it “teleports” nanite medical teams into bodies during healings, relocates the channel bodily in the Zen1 adventure, and promises “intergalactic stargate, teleportation of bioship craft” for civilian use after first contact. The mechanism given is the archive’s usual one — a quantum reality in which distance, like time, is a parameter rather than a wall.

Teleportation is the archive’s measure of the withheld future: a mode of travel it insists has existed since the Nixon administration, waiting — with free energy and the Replicators — for the disclosure that hands it over.
